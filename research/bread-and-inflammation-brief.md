# Bread, Fermentation, and Inflammation — A Personal Brief

> A one-page synthesis for Gina on whether sourdough vs. unleavened flatbread matters for managing SFN, MCAS, and hEDS-driven inflammation, especially during a flare.

**Prepared:** June 28, 2026
**Disclaimer:** Research summary, not medical advice. Individual response varies. Trial period and self-tracking via the Flare Log will tell you more than any study.

---

## Bottom line up front

**For your specific picture — biopsy-confirmed SFN, MCAS overlap, hEDS, autoimmune progesterone dermatitis — a well-fermented sourdough is likely a meaningfully lower inflammatory load than an unleavened flatbread made from the same wheat flour, *provided* the fermentation is long enough (12+ hours) and includes active lactic acid bacteria.**

The mechanism is not gluten. It's three things you can actually feel: **FODMAPs, ATIs, and protein/starch digestibility.** Unleavened bread skips all three of these benefits, because there's no microbial fermentation step.

This does not mean unleavened bread is "bad." It means that for someone with a documented multi-system immune phenotype, the fermentation step is doing free anti-inflammatory work that an unleavened bread doesn't get.

---

## Three mechanisms that matter for your phenotype

### 1. ATIs (amylase-trypsin inhibitors) — the actually-important wheat protein

ATIs are 2–4% of wheat protein. They are *not* gluten. They are pest-defense proteins that activate **TLR4 receptors on myeloid immune cells**, driving release of TNF-α, IL-6, and MCP-1 — the same inflammatory cytokines that sensitize and damage small nerve fibers in SFN, and the same ones implicated in mast cell activation.

- ATIs are heat-resistant and protease-resistant — baking alone does not destroy them ([J Exp Med, 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3526354/), [Schuppan group](https://www.genengnews.com/news/ati-proteins-found-associated-with-inflammation-of-chronic-health-disorders/)).
- ATIs have been shown in animal models to **exacerbate CNS inflammation** in experimental MS ([Gut, 2024](https://gut.bmj.com/content/73/1/5)).
- ATIs have been linked to inflammation in **rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, MS, NCWS, and fatty liver disease** — all immune-mediated conditions in the same family as your phenotype ([GEN News, 2016](https://www.genengnews.com/news/ati-proteins-found-associated-with-inflammation-of-chronic-health-disorders/)).
- **Sourdough fermentation degrades ATIs by 41% at 12 hours of fermentation** ([ScienceDirect, 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0733521022001631)) and reduces pro-inflammatory TNF-α and MCP-1 release from human immune cells by ~50% ([PubMed, 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32708800/), [Frontiers in Allergy, 2023](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/allergy/articles/10.3389/falgy.2023.1228353/full)).
- Unleavened bread retains essentially full ATI activity. Same wheat, no fermentation = same TLR4 activation.

**For your SFN-MCAS loop specifically:** ATIs activate the *same myeloid cells* that release the inflammatory mediators driving neurogenic mast cell activation. Reducing ATI load is a direct intervention on the loop.

### 2. FODMAPs — the GI and mast-cell load

FODMAPs (fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides, polyols) — especially fructans in wheat — drive GI symptoms in IBS and NCWS, and can contribute to mast cell activation in MCAS patients.

- **Sourdough fermentation reduces fructans by up to 90% at 4.5+ hours of fermentation**, with 12-hour fermentation cutting them by 64–69% in standard wheat ([ScienceDirect, 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0733521022001631), [Foods, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11172170/), [Frontiers, 2018](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/microbiology/articles/10.3389/fmicb.2018.01972/full)).
- Cold retard (your 10–12 hour fridge step) extends bacterial activity time and adds to this reduction.
- Unleavened bread keeps all the FODMAPs intact.

**For you specifically:** with gastroparesis and chronic urticaria on your problem list, lower FODMAPs likely means fewer GI flares, less mast cell triggering, and reduced systemic load.

### 3. Glycemic index — the inflammation-via-blood-sugar pathway

Postprandial glucose spikes drive oxidative stress and pro-inflammatory cytokine release. Chronic mild hyperglycemia is independently linked to small fiber neuropathy progression even in non-diabetic patients.

- **Typical white bread GI: 71–100. Whole-wheat non-sourdough GI: 71. Sourdough wheat bread GI: ~54** ([Cleveland Clinic](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/benefits-of-sourdough-bread), [Good In Bread](https://wearegoodinbread.com/sourdough-lower-glycaemic-index/), [Foods, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11172170/)).
- Cold-retarded sourdough can drop GI further — some studies show GI 40–43 with extended LAB fermentation ([Academia, 2021](https://www.academia.edu/61365339/)).
- Mechanism: lactic acid–starch interactions slow gastric emptying and reduce starch hydrolysis rate ([PubMed, 2009](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0733521009000307)).
- Unleavened white-flour flatbread is in the same GI range as white bread (~70–100). Whole-grain unleavened sits ~60–70.

**For you:** your A1C is currently normal per the VA labs, but your autonomic system is already taxed. Smaller glucose excursions = less autonomic strain.

### 4. Protein digestibility — a bonus

Sourdough fermentation increases in vitro protein digestibility from ~64% (baker's yeast bread) to ~74–80% ([Frigerio thesis, IRIS](https://iris.uniroma1.it/bitstream/11573/1760001/1/Tesi_dottorato_Frigerio.pdf), [Foods, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11172170/)). Pre-broken-down peptides means less immune-system work in the gut. Unleavened bread = full intact wheat proteins.

## What the science does NOT support

- Sourdough is **not gluten-free.** It does not make wheat safe for celiac patients. LAB fermentation reduces gluten peptides but not below the 20-ppm threshold required for celiac safety ([Microbiology Research, 2025](https://doaj.org/article/d56ac79473334eb8bab00401fe541c41)).
- Sourdough's GI is "low-to-medium," not low. ~54 vs. the <50 threshold for true low-GI.
- The "sourdough fixes everything" claims online are overstated. The effect is real, but modest in absolute terms — meaningful for a chronic inflammatory phenotype, probably negligible for a healthy person.
- Commercial "sourdough" with added baker's yeast and short fermentation does *not* get these benefits. The microbial work has to actually happen — long bulk + cold retard + active LAB starter. **Your loaf qualifies.**

## Direct implications for flare management

### During a flare (yellow or red day)
- Sourdough wheat bread is likely a lower inflammatory load than unleavened wheat flatbread.
- Both are inferior to a fully grain-free meal during a severe flare. The grain question only matters once you've decided you're eating grain.
- The single biggest lever is *quantity*, not type. Two slices of sourdough during a flare is gentler on the system than four slices of any kind.

### On a green or stable day
- Your sourdough is a genuinely well-designed food for your phenotype.
- Cold retard maximizes the benefits — every hour of cold fermentation extends bacterial enzyme activity time. Your 10–12 hour retard is on the right end of this curve.
- If you ever want to go further: a 24-hour cold retard reduces ATIs and FODMAPs even more. Diminishing returns past ~36 hours.

### If unleavened bread is what you have
- Eat smaller portions.
- Pair with fat and protein to lower the effective GI.
- Avoid during flares if possible.
- Older wheat varieties (einkorn, emmer, spelt) have lower ATI content than modern wheat ([Kate Scarlata blog](https://blog.katescarlata.com/2017/02/14/heard-amylase-trypsin-inhibitors/)) — relevant for any unleavened bread you choose to eat.

## What this means for the body-os flare log

This is testable data, not just literature. To know whether the science maps onto *your* body:

- **Tag bread type in your Flare Log notes:** "sourdough (cold-retard)" vs. "commercial bread" vs. "unleavened" vs. "no bread."
- Watch for differences in: itch storm frequency, GI symptoms, autonomic flares, fatigue, post-meal energy.
- After 4–6 weeks of consistent tagging, your data will tell you whether the literature generalizes to you.

**Your body is the only n=1 study that matters.** The literature gives you a hypothesis. The Flare Log tests it.
